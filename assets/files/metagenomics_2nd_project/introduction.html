Introduction

Metagenomics is the study of microbial communities at the genome level. Their genetic materials are obtained in situ (Bragg and Tyson, 2014). This direct sequencing of the genetic materials from their own environment is the key advantage of metagenomics because it bypasses the culturing step, which has limited the scope of microorganism research as 99% of microorganisms do not survive in a conventional culture environment (Handelsman, 2004). The advance in sequencing technology has allowed researchers to discover possible pathogens or probiotics for some diseases such as inflammatory bowel disease (Peterson et al., 2012 and Wang et al., 2015). This recent success in metagenomics quickly grabbed the interest of many public and private investors (Reardon, 2016).

Another reason why this field of research became very attractive is that microorganisms thrive everywhere. Specifically in our body, we have on average 70 trillion cells living and 40 trillion cells are microorganisms (Abbott, 2016). Thus, this abundance of microorganisms in our body allowed human microbiome research, which is a community of microorganisms living in association with human cells, to have a wide range of disease applications: cancer, diabetes, infectious disease, and immunology(Greenblum et al., 2011; Walker et al., 2015)

One popular instance of microbiome research is an identification of differentially abundant operational taxonomic units (OTUs) on disease cases when compared to healthy controls. Recent studies found a significant difference in gut microbiome composition between diabetes patients and healthy controls (Larsen et al., 2010 and Qin et al., 2012).

However, microorganisms interact with the host and also between themselves, and these interactions can give an additional layer of information that the popular approach lacked. Thus, research on these interactions can give rise to an interesting and possibly different conclusion. Previous studies on these interactions created association networks using correlation measures where the edges are correlation coefficients or p values to indicate statistically (and potentially biologically) significant interactions and the nodes are the microorganisms (Weiss et al., 2016). This approach infers a co-occurrence network for a given microbiome.

Here, a novel method for creating association network is presented. This method is a new extension to an existing differential network inference method (Fairclough et al., unpublished). Rather than generating a differential network between two data sets, the new method just uses one data set and infers a co-occurrence network using proportionality measures and random permutation. To validate this methodology, it was compared with several other existing network inference methods for sensitivity, specificity, and precision. Also, the methodology was successfully applied to a case-control study of stroke patients, a data set downloaded from EBI metagenomics database (Yin et al. , 2015; Mitchell et al.). The resulting network successfully retrieved some microorganisms known to be associated with stroke and also highlighted some potential interactions. Thus, this methodology can be used as a hypothesis generator to detect any (statistically) significantly co-occurring microorganisms in a given data set.
